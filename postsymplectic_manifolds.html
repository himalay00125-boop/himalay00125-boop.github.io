What is a Symplectic Manifold?

26 April, 2025

Classical mechanics begins with a phase space: positions and momenta of a system living together in a single geometric object. For a particle moving in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the phase space is $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, with coordinates $(q_1, \ldots, q_n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$. What makes this space special is not its dimension but a particular geometric structure it carries — one that tells us how positions and momenta are intertwined. That structure is the symplectic form, and the manifolds that carry it are the subject of symplectic geometry.

In this note I want to explain what a symplectic manifold is, give the canonical examples, and say something about why the two defining conditions — closedness and non-degeneracy — are not mere technicalities but load-bearing parts of the theory.

The Definition

Definition 1.1 — Symplectic Manifold

A symplectic manifold is a pair $(M, \omega)$ where $M$ is a smooth manifold of even dimension $2n$ and $\omega \in \Omega^2(M)$ is a differential 2-form satisfying:

(i) Closed: $d\omega = 0$,

(ii) Non-degenerate: for every $p \in M$ and every nonzero $v \in T_pM$, there exists $w \in T_pM$ such that $\omega_p(v, w) \neq 0$.

The form $\omega$ is called the symplectic form. Non-degeneracy is the condition that $\omega$ is a perfect pairing on each tangent space: the map $v \mapsto \omega(v, \cdot)$ from $T_pM$ to $T_p^*M$ is an isomorphism. This forces $M$ to be even-dimensional — an odd-dimensional manifold cannot carry a non-degenerate 2-form.

The Canonical Example

The most fundamental example is $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ with coordinates $(q_1, \ldots, q_n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$, equipped with the standard symplectic form:

$$\omega_0 = \sum_{i=1}^n dq_i \wedge dp_i.$$

This is closed because it has constant coefficients, and it is non-degenerate because in the basis $\{\partial_{q_i}, \partial_{p_i}\}$ it takes the matrix form

$$\Omega = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix},$$

which is clearly invertible. This matrix $\Omega$ is the one that appears throughout classical mechanics — it encodes the Poisson bracket, Hamilton's equations, and the structure of canonical transformations all at once.

Example 1.2 — The Cotangent Bundle

If $Q$ is any smooth $n$-dimensional manifold (the configuration space), its cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ carries a canonical symplectic form. There is a tautological 1-form $\lambda \in \Omega^1(T^*Q)$, defined by $\lambda_{(q,p)}(v) = p(\pi_* v)$ where $\pi: T^*Q \to Q$ is the projection. Then $\omega = -d\lambda$ is symplectic. In local coordinates, $\lambda = \sum_i p_i \, dq_i$ and $\omega = \sum_i dq_i \wedge dp_i$, recovering the standard form.

Cotangent bundles are the natural phase spaces of classical mechanics. The configuration space $Q$ encodes positions; the fibre over each point encodes momenta. The symplectic form is not an additional structure imposed on this space — it emerges canonically from the geometry of $T^*Q$ itself.

Why Closedness?

Non-degeneracy has a clear mechanical interpretation: it says that positions and momenta are genuinely paired, that no direction in phase space is invisible to $\omega$. But why require $d\omega = 0$?

One answer comes from Darboux's theorem, which is the foundational local result of symplectic geometry.

Theorem 2.1 — Darboux

Let $(M, \omega)$ be a symplectic manifold and $p \in M$. Then there exist local coordinates $(q_1, \ldots, q_n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ near $p$ in which $$\omega = \sum_{i=1}^n dq_i \wedge dp_i.$$ In particular, all symplectic manifolds of the same dimension are locally symplectomorphic.

Darboux's theorem is striking: unlike Riemannian geometry, symplectic geometry has no local invariants. You cannot distinguish two symplectic manifolds of the same dimension by any local measurement. All the interesting geometry is global. The proof uses the Moser trick — a deformation argument that requires $d\omega = 0$ in an essential way to construct the interpolating vector fields. Without closedness, Darboux fails and local invariants appear.

Symplectic geometry is, in a precise sense, the geometry of global phenomena. Locally there is nothing to see.

Non-degeneracy and the Volume Form

Non-degeneracy has a beautiful global consequence. If $\dim M = 2n$, the $n$-fold wedge product

$$\omega^n = \underbrace{\omega \wedge \cdots \wedge \omega}_{n \text{ times}}$$

is a nowhere-vanishing top-degree form on $M$ — a volume form. This means every symplectic manifold is orientable, and if $M$ is compact, it satisfies

$$\int_M \omega^n \neq 0.$$

This is a strong topological constraint. It implies, for instance, that the even-dimensional cohomology groups $H^{2k}(M; \mathbb{R})$ are all non-trivial for $0 \leq k \leq n$ — the symplectic form produces non-zero cohomology classes $[\omega], [\omega^2], \ldots, [\omega^n]$. Spheres $S^{2n}$ for $n \geq 2$ fail this: $H^2(S^{2n}; \mathbb{R}) = 0$, so they carry no symplectic structure.

Remark 2.2

The sphere $S^2$ is the exception: it does carry a symplectic form, namely the standard area form. It is the only sphere that does. This is one reason $S^2$ plays a distinguished role in symplectic topology.

Hamilton's Equations Revisited

The symplectic form provides the language in which classical mechanics is most naturally stated. Given a smooth function $H: M \to \mathbb{R}$ (the Hamiltonian), non-degeneracy of $\omega$ guarantees the existence of a unique vector field $X_H$ satisfying

$$\iota_{X_H} \omega = dH,$$

where $\iota_{X_H}\omega$ denotes the interior product. This is the Hamiltonian vector field of $H$. In Darboux coordinates, the flow of $X_H$ satisfies exactly

$$\dot{q}_i = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i},$$

which are Hamilton's equations. The symplectic form is not a convenient notation here — it is the object that makes the passage from $H$ to its equations of motion well-defined on any manifold, not just $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$.

Looking Ahead

What I find compelling about symplectic geometry is that it sits at an unusual intersection. On one side, it encodes the structure of classical mechanics with complete precision. On the other, its global questions — when can one symplectic manifold be embedded in another? how rigid are symplectomorphisms? — turn out to be extraordinarily hard and to connect with the deepest parts of topology and analysis.

In future posts I want to explore symplectic capacities and embedding problems, and then turn to how symplectic ideas are entering data science through structure-preserving neural networks. Both directions start here, with this definition and its two conditions.
