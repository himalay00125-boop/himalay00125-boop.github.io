Teaching a market to move like a pendulum

June 2026  ·  Himanshu Mishra

There is a diagram I keep coming back to when I think about why most trading models feel wrong to me. You have a horizontal axis for time and a vertical axis for price, and somewhere on that plot a wiggly line makes its way from left to right. The line goes up for a while, then down, then sideways. Every indicator ever invented — every moving average, every RSI, every Bollinger Band, every LSTM that's been trained on market data — lives in that diagram. They are all functions of that wiggly line, or transformations of it, or attempts to predict where it will go next. The price axis is all there is.

That bothers me, and I've spent a while trying to understand exactly why. A market is not just price. It is price and volume, together, in each bar, and the relationship between the two changes constantly in ways that carry meaning. A bar that closes up 3% on ten times normal volume is not the same event as a bar that closes up 3% on half normal volume, but if you draw both as points on the price axis they are indistinguishable. The standard diagram throws away half the data before the modelling even begins.

I wanted to know what you could see if you stopped throwing it away.

The pendulum thing

When a physicist wants to understand a pendulum, they don't just plot the angle over time. They plot the angle against the angular velocity — position against momentum — and what they get is an ellipse. The whole life of the pendulum, every swing it will ever make, is encoded in that ellipse. The size of the ellipse tells you the energy. The shape tells you whether the motion is periodic or chaotic. Two pendulums with the same amplitude but different periods will trace different ellipses, and you can read that difference off the geometry directly.

This is phase space, and it is one of the most powerful ideas in classical mechanics. The position and momentum of a system together define a point in a two-dimensional plane, and the history of the system is a continuous trajectory through that plane. Different physical systems have characteristic trajectory shapes — ellipses, spirals, strange attractors — and the geometry of those shapes tells you things that you would never see in a one-dimensional time series.

I started wondering whether you could do the same thing to a market. What would it look like to treat price as position and something volume-like as momentum, and then watch the trajectory that traces out?

What actually happens when you try it

The encoding I settled on uses q(t) = ln P(t) for position and p(t) = V(t) · sign(ΔP(t)) for momentum: the log-price, and the bar's volume signed positive if the close was above the open and negative if it was below. The sign convention is the key thing — it means the momentum coordinate captures not just how much activity there was but what direction it was pushing. You take a rolling window of these (q, p) pairs and plot them, and you get a cloud.

The first time I plotted one of these clouds on real market data I found it more interesting than I expected. During a clean uptrend the cloud sits in a strip tilted toward the upper right: prices are ascending along the q axis and the volume is consistently positive on the p axis. During consolidation it collapses into a compact ball near the centre, with the p axis churning back and forth between positive and negative but not going anywhere. During a sudden selloff the cloud lurches downward and to the left, and the new points appear far from the old ones before the cloud slowly recentres. You can see regime changes happen, in real time, as the cloud morphs.

The natural question was whether you could quantify that. The shape is clearly informative — but shape is not a number. I needed a scalar that captured something essential about the shape so I could track it, threshold it, and use it in a model.

Borrowing Gromov width from topology

This is where symplectic geometry comes in, and I want to be clear that I didn't arrive here by design. I came to it because I was already thinking about symplectic manifolds for other reasons — questions about when one symplectic space can be embedded into another, which is the kind of pure mathematics that has nothing obviously to do with finance. But symplectic geometry is, at its core, the geometry of the (q, p) plane, and when I realised I was literally working with position-momentum coordinates on a plane the connection became hard to ignore.

The symplectic capacity of a domain in the (q, p) plane is a measure of how much of that plane the domain occupies, in a way that is invariant under all the transformations that preserve the symplectic structure. The simplest version of it — the one I use — is just the area of the convex hull of the domain. In the mathematical literature this is related to the Gromov width, which is the radius of the largest symplectic ball that embeds into the domain. For my purposes what matters is that it is a well-defined, geometrically motivated number that I can compute from the rolling point cloud at every bar, and that it has a formal stability property: its variation is bounded by a Lipschitz condition that links the change in capacity to the size of the perturbation applied to the cloud.

In practice this means that when capacity is varying smoothly, the market is in a well-behaved regime. When it suddenly spikes, something about the current bar has violated the stability bound — the market has done something that doesn't fit the recent pattern, structurally — and the right response is to suspend predictions rather than double down. I track this capacity as C(t), maintain its rolling 95th percentile, and trigger an ALERT whenever it crosses that threshold. I also track the perimeter of the hull as a secondary feature, because a long thin cloud and a round compact cloud can have the same area but very different implications for what the market is doing.

Then the shape got more complicated

Somewhere in the middle of building this I started wondering whether the convex hull was the right summary of the cloud's geometry. The hull tells you about the outer boundary; it says nothing about what's going on inside. And sometimes what's going on inside is interesting. I started noticing cases where the cloud would fragment — split into two distinct clusters with a gap between them — in ways that seemed to correspond to the market being genuinely uncertain, pulled between two competing narratives. The hull area was the same as in a unified cloud, but the structure was completely different.

This led me to persistent homology, which is a technique from topological data analysis for describing the shape of a point cloud in terms of its connected components and loops. The basic idea is that you grow balls around each point, increasing the radius, and watch the topology change. Components merge; cycles form and then fill in. Each topological event gets recorded with the radius at which it was born and the radius at which it died, and the difference — its lifetime — tells you how significant the feature was. Noise produces lots of short-lived features. Real structure produces features with long lifetimes.

From the rolling cloud I extract the number of long-lived connected components — which tells you how fragmented the distribution is — and the number of long-lived loops — which tells you whether there's cyclical, closed-orbit behaviour in the current window. Both go into the model as features alongside the capacity and perimeter. It means the model's input is a description of the market that encodes not just how big the cloud is but how connected it is and whether it has holes in it. I found that more satisfying than adding another derivative of price.

A model that cannot cheat

I thought carefully about the learning setup because there are a lot of ways to accidentally build a model that cheats. Any time you touch the future — normalising over the whole dataset, training on data that includes bars you haven't reached yet, computing statistics that require looking ahead — you are giving the model information it wouldn't have had in real trading. The backtested results will look good and the live results will be disappointing, and the gap between them is entirely explained by the leakage.

The forecaster avoids this by being a pure online learner. At each bar it takes the features computed from data available up to that bar, uses the current model to make a prediction, evaluates the previous prediction against what actually happened, and updates its weights. That's the complete loop. The weights at any step are a function only of the observations that preceded it. There is no training phase that precedes trading — the model starts from scratch and gets smarter with every bar it sees.

The ensemble I use pairs a Passive-Aggressive Regressor with a Hoeffding Adaptive Tree. The first is a linear model with a deliberately conservative update rule: it ignores small errors and responds strongly to large ones, which makes it stable when the market is predictable and reactive when it isn't. The second is a decision tree designed for data streams, one that uses statistical tests to decide when new splits are warranted and drift detection to restructure itself when the distribution shifts. Together they give a forecast that is neither too rigid to adapt nor too plastic to be consistent, and the HOLD signal activates whenever the ensemble lacks the confidence to commit.

Three paths forward

I was careful about not pretending to more certainty than the model actually has. Rather than outputting a single predicted price, the forecaster generates three five-step-ahead paths: a base path from the raw ensemble prediction, and bull and bear paths that expand outward by a factor of the Lipschitz stability bound. This means the width of the scenario fan is determined by the current geometry of the market — wide when the phase-space cloud is large and uncertain, narrow when it is compact and well-behaved. The uncertainty is structural, not a fixed error bar.

The thing I actually learned

Building this tool taught me something I didn't fully expect. The symplectic geometry, as a framework, is genuinely good at one specific thing: detecting when a market is in a stable regime versus when it is transitioning between regimes. The capacity measure tracks this beautifully and the ALERT system, in testing, catches a lot of the moments when confident predictions would have been wrong. The topology adds sensitivity to internal structure that the geometry misses.

Whether the ensemble is a good predictor of direction when it does commit — that is a harder question, and one I have not answered with the rigour it deserves. An honest evaluation requires out-of-sample data collected after the model was finalised, real or simulated capital, and a long enough window to distinguish skill from luck. I haven't done that. What I've done is build a framework that I believe is mathematically well-motivated and practically honest — it knows when it doesn't know, which is more than most systems I've seen — and then made the code available for anyone who wants to test it properly.
